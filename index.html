Workshop on extracting structured knowledge from scientific publications (ESSP)

The ever-increasing growth rate of the body of scientific literature demands automatic methods for the timely and automatic extraction of information from scientific publications. Structured information can be extracted at different levels of granularity. Previous and ongoing work has focussed on bibliographic information (segmentation and linking of referenced literature, Wick et al., 2013), keyword extraction and categorization (e.g., what are tasks, materials and processes central to a publication, Augenstein et al., 2017), and cataloguing research findings. Scientific discoveries can often be represented as pairwise relationships, e.g., protein-protein (Mallory et al. 2015), drug-drug (Segura-Bedmar et al., 2013), and chemical-disease (Li et. al, 2016) interactions, or as more complicated networks such as action graphs describing scientific procedures (e.g., synthesis recipes in material sciences, Mysore et al., 2017). Information extracted with such methods can be enriched with time-stamps, and other meta-information, such as indicators of uncertainty or limitations of the discovered facts (Zhou et al., 2015). Structured representations, such as knowledge graphs, summarize information from a variety of sources in a convenient and machine readable format. Graph representations, that link the information of a large body of publications, can reveal patterns and lead to the discovery of new information that would not be apparent from the analysis of just one publication. This kind of aggregation can lead to new scientific insights (Kim et. al, 2017), and it can also help to detect trends (Prabhakaran et al., 2016), or find experts for a particular scientific area (Mahmood et. al, 2017). While various workshops have focused separately on several aspects — extraction of information from scientific articles, building and using knowledge graphs, the analysis of bibliographical information, graph algorithms for text analysis — the proposed workshop focuses on processing scientific articles and creating structured repositories such as knowledge graphs for finding new information and making scientific discoveries.


We invite submission on (but not limited to) the following topics:
  • building knowledge graphs from scientific publications
    • identifying concepts in scientific articles (in various domains)
    • relations in scientific articles (in various domains) — including n-ary relations with n>2, “negative relations”
    • large scale information extraction from scientific publications
    • targeted information extraction for completing knowledge graphs
    • updating knowledge graphs (not just adding new info, removing erroneous facts, or possibly having explicit links for incorrect statements)
    • finding patterns and mining new information in knowledge graphs
  • using knowledge graphs
    • finding new scientific hypotheses
    • visualization of knowledge in particular domains
    • tools for interacting with users
    • querying KGs
  • evaluation of extracted knowledge
